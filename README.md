He never went by any standard Anglo-Saxon name. His full name — the one he wanted people to recognize him by, written on every informal contract, every handshake agreement, every whispered introduction in the back rooms of places that did not advertise their existence — was Camel Boy "Kill Everyone" Shanking Teen.

The name had layers. *Camel Boy* came from his youth, from a nickname bestowed with the careless cruelty children practice on one another, a reference to his endurance, his ability to go without — without food, without approval, without belonging. *Kill Everyone* was the middle name he had chosen for himself at seventeen, not out of genuine menace, but out of a young man's desperate need to be taken seriously, to erect walls before anyone thought to climb them. And *Shanking Teen* — that was the surname that had followed him from his developmental years into adulthood, a reputation crystallized into nomenclature, a reminder that the world had first known him as dangerous before it knew him as anything else.

He was a full-grown man now. That was the irony of *Teen* trailing behind him everywhere.

Some of his ancestry was Moorish — the particular strain that had crossed water and time and conquest and mixed quietly into the fabric of Southern Europe. But looking at him, one would have said Southern Gaul without hesitation. Curly hair that moved when the wind moved. Olive-colored skin that shifted warmth with the seasons. Camel Boy was not of Hispania by birth. He was of Gaullia. But during these years of pertinence — these years that people who documented such things would later mark as *significant* — he had grown increasingly disenfranchised from the Gaullic establishment.

It did not help that he was an in-betweener. It did not help that he was adopted.

The Gaullic establishment had a particular texture to its rejection. It was not loud. It did not arrive with slurs or locked doors. It arrived instead in the form of slow exclusions — committees he was never invited to join, decisions made before he entered the room, the sense that regardless of his capability, his belonging was conditional and would remain so. He was useful when useful, ornamental when ornamental, and invisible when the math of power required it.

So when Hispania came to him — or when he came to Hispania, depending on who was telling the story — the transition was not surprising to those who knew him. It was, if anything, overdue.
